## MR – Interpretation of Phase Diagrams (Concept Summary)

This question is a **multiple-response** item about how to correctly interpret **binary eutectic phase diagrams**. It presents four statements and asks which are correct. Each statement targets a specific concept:

---

### 1. Meaning of the eutectic point

- In a **binary eutectic system**, the **eutectic point** corresponds to:
  - **One specific composition** (the eutectic composition).
  - **One specific temperature** (the eutectic temperature).
- At this point, the equilibrium reaction is:
  - $( L \leftrightarrow \alpha + \beta )$
  - i.e. **one liquid phase transforms into two solid phases** simultaneously.
- Common mistake: saying the liquid transforms into **one** solid phase; the correct statement must explicitly say **two solid phases**.

---

### 2. Reading phases and amounts from the Cu–Ag phase diagram

- Example: **Cu–Ag eutectic diagram**, alloy with **20 wt% Ag at 800 °C**.
- Steps:
  - Locate the point (20 wt% Ag, 800 °C) on the diagram.
  - This point lies in the **α + L** region → the alloy consists of:
    - **α solid (Cu-rich solid solution)**  
    - **Liquid (L)**
- Use a **tie line** at 800 °C:
  - Left intersection with the phase boundary → composition of **α**.
  - Right intersection → composition of **L**.
- Apply the **lever rule**:
  - Because the overall composition (20 wt% Ag) lies closer to the liquid composition than to the α composition, the **arm on the liquid side is shorter**.
  - Shorter arm → **smaller fraction of liquid** and **larger fraction of α**.
- Conclusion: at 20 wt% Ag and 800 °C, the alloy is **two-phase (α + L)** with a **greater mass fraction of α solid** than liquid.

---

### 3. Roles of the liquidus and solidus lines

- **Liquidus line**:
  - Boundary between **all-liquid** and **liquid + solid**.
  - Above the liquidus → only **liquid** is stable.
  - Just below the liquidus → **liquid + solid** coexist.
- **Solidus line**:
  - Boundary between **liquid + solid** and **all solid**.
  - Just above the solidus → **liquid + solid**.
  - Below the solidus → completely **solid**.
- Common mistake: saying the **liquidus** separates “two-phase solid–liquid” from “solid only.”  
  - Correct version: it is the **solidus** that separates “solid + liquid” from “solid only.”

---

### 4. When the lever rule applies (equilibrium vs non-equilibrium)

- **Lever rule**:
  - Used to calculate **mass fractions of phases** in a **two-phase region**.
  - Assumes:
    - **Equilibrium conditions**.
    - Uniform phase compositions given exactly by the tie-line end points.
- **Cored (multi-layered) structures**:
  - Form during **non-equilibrium cooling** (e.g. relatively fast cooling).
  - Atoms do **not** have enough time to diffuse and homogenize.
  - Composition **varies within a grain** (center vs edges), producing concentric compositional “rings.”
- Because cored structures are **non-equilibrium**, the simple lever-rule calculation (based on equilibrium tie lines) **does not accurately describe** the detailed internal distribution of composition within these grains.
- Correct interpretation:
  - Lever rule → **equilibrium two-phase regions**.
  - Not valid for predicting the internal layering of **non-equilibrium cored microstructures**.

---

### Big picture: what the slide is reinforcing

- How to correctly interpret key features of a **binary eutectic phase diagram**:
  - What happens at the **eutectic point**.
  - How to determine **phases present** and **relative phase amounts** at a given \( C_0, T \).
  - The **distinct roles** of the **liquidus** and **solidus** lines.
  - The **limitations** of the **lever rule**, especially when cooling conditions are **non-equilibrium**.
